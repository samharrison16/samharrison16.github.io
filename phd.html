About the research

My PhD, Trees on farms: ecological and socioeconomic analyses of tropical agroforestry landscapes using remote sensing, used geospatial analysis to study both the ecological and socioeconomic dimensions of agroforestry — the trees that farmers retain, plant, and manage within cultivated landscapes across the tropics.

I developed methods for monitoring aspects of biodiversity spatially in tropical mosaic agroforestry landscapes, working from individual farm plots up to national scales. As part of this, I carried out spatial cropland data product assessments to improve agricultural monitoring, including evaluation against the FAO Land Cover Classification System (LCCS). I also linked geospatial data to census and household survey data to understand the socioeconomic factors shaping the abundance of trees on farms in Uganda — connecting what satellites can see to the decisions farming households actually make.

The thesis was supervised by Casey Ryan and Gary Watmough (University of Edinburgh) and Rhett Harrison (CIFOR-ICRAF), and funded by NERC in partnership with CIFOR-ICRAF.
