About the project

SECO is a collaborative research project working to resolve long-standing uncertainties in the carbon cycle of tropical dry forests and savannas. These ecosystems cover more of the tropics than humid rainforest, but their carbon stocks and the rates at which they change are far less well quantified — partly because dense canopy methods developed for wet forest don't transfer well to drier, more open vegetation structures.

My role is to lead spatial modelling of carbon stocks and fluxes across these landscapes. I integrate L-band SAR time series with field plot inventory data in spatial and statistical models, producing biomass maps and quantifying biomass change over time. This involves substantial work on data management and QA/QC across field and remote sensing datasets, and building reproducible analytical workflows in R and Python that the wider project team can rely on and build from.

The aim is to move from patchy, locally-calibrated estimates toward biomass and biomass-change maps with properly resolved uncertainty — output that can meaningfully inform both carbon science and on-the-ground land management decisions in dry tropical regions.
