About

I'm an environmental research scientist and geospatial analyst based at the University of Edinburgh, where I work at the intersection of remote sensing, ecology, and carbon accounting. My research focuses on tropical dry forests and savannas — ecosystems that are chronically under-monitored relative to humid tropical forest, despite covering a greater share of the world's tropical land area and holding large, uncertain carbon stocks.

I currently lead spatial modelling of carbon stocks and fluxes across the dry tropics for the SECO project, integrating L-band SAR time series with field inventory data to build biomass maps and quantify carbon change over time. Before this, my PhD examined the ecological and socioeconomic dimensions of tropical agroforestry landscapes, linking satellite-derived measures of tree cover to household survey data across East Africa.

Alongside my academic research, I provide technical assistance to carbon project developers working under voluntary carbon market standards such as Plan Vivo — helping translate remote sensing and field measurement protocols into practical, auditable monitoring systems. I'm motivated by research that moves beyond publication into real decisions about land management, and by building analytical workflows that are rigorous enough to trust and simple enough for others to pick up.
